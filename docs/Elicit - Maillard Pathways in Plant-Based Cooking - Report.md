# Maillard reaction in Plant-based cooking.

The literature discusses Maillard reaction pathways in plant-based meat alternatives progressing through three main stages: early-stage condensation forming Schiff bases and Amadori products, intermediate-stage degradation producing heterocyclic compounds and melanoidins, and advanced-stage formation of AGEs and heterocyclic amines, along with additional pathways including dehydroalanine formation and myoglobin-catalyzed reactions that transform amino acids and catalyze lipid oxidation interactions.

## Abstract

Maillard reaction pathways in plant-based meat alternatives progress through three distinct stages during cooking and processing. The early stage involves condensation reactions between carbonyl groups of reducing carbohydrates and ε-amino groups of lysine, forming Schiff bases that rearrange to Amadori products \[1, 2\]. The intermediate stage features Amadori product degradation through dehydration and fission, producing reductone intermediates, heterocyclic compounds, and melanoidins with beneficial antioxidant and antimicrobial properties \[1, 3, 4\]. The advanced stage generates melanoidins and advanced glycation end-products \[1\], with cooking at 160-250°C forming heterocyclic amines (160.30-381.30 ng/g total) \[5\] and acrylamide \[3, 4\], though plant-based alternatives accumulate lower heterocyclic amine levels than conventional meat \[5\]. Additional pathways include dehydroalanine formation \[6\] and myoglobin-catalyzed reactions where heme cofactors transform amino acids, nucleotides, vitamins, and sugars into flavor compounds while catalyzing lipid oxidation that interacts with Maillard intermediates \[7, 7\].

Pathway progression depends critically on processing conditions and ingredients. Controlled glycation at 40-80°C with slightly alkaline pH and water activity of 0.5-0.8 produces beneficial early-stage products improving protein solubility, emulsification, and thermal stability \[1, 1, 4\]. Higher cooking temperatures (160-250°C) during consumer preparation inevitably progress reactions to advanced stages forming harmful products \[5, 7\]. Plant protein structure influences pathways through amino acid profiles, with proteins rich in lysine, leucine, aspartic acid, and arginine exhibiting characteristic reactivity patterns \[1\], while pre-processing via extrusion alters reactive site accessibility \[5\]. Myoglobin addition at 0.5-1.0% catalyzes pathway enhancement, increasing pyrazine content from 11.9% to 16.1% and aldehydes while decreasing hydrocarbons \[7, 7\]. Control strategies including competing amino acids, bivalent cations, phytochemicals, and optimized pH can limit harmful advanced-stage product formation while preserving functional benefits \[4\].

## Reaction Stages and Sequential Pathways

The Maillard reaction in plant-based meat alternatives progresses through distinct stages with characteristic chemical transformations. The early stage involves condensation reactions between carbonyl groups of reducing carbohydrates and ε-amino groups of lysine, forming Schiff bases \[1\]. These Schiff bases subsequently rearrange to Amadori products, representing key early intermediates \[1\]. The involvement of available lysine and sugars in this initial reaction stage is critical for determining subsequent pathway progression \[2\].

The intermediate stage features degradation of Amadori products through dehydration and fission reactions, leading to formation of dicarbonyl compounds \[1\]. This stage produces bioactive Maillard reaction products with potential functional benefits \[4\]. Reductone intermediates, heterocyclic compounds, and melanoidins form during this phase \[3\]. Studies tracking reaction progression found that higher Maillard reaction marker levels were present in uncooked plant-based meat alternatives, with lower levels forming during cooking compared to conventional meat \[6\].

The advanced stage involves further decomposition and polymerization, forming melanoidins and advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) \[1, 3\]. During plant-based burger cooking at 160°C for 6 minutes, protein-bound heterocyclic amines like MeAαC accumulated from 29.23 ± 3.50 to 59.44 ± 0.26 ng/g \[5\], though prolonged heating to 12 minutes decreased MeAαC content to 42.26 ± 0.11 ng/g \[5\], suggesting degradation or transformation pathways at extended heating durations. Thermal home-processing led to evident AGE accumulation \[5\].

Specific pathways identified included the dehydroalanine pathway, which was analyzed alongside traditional Maillard reaction markers \[6\]. Myoglobin-catalyzed reactions represent an additional pathway relevant to plant-based meat alternatives, where myoglobin unfolds and its heme cofactor catalyzes transformation of amino acids, nucleotides, vitamins, and sugars into flavor and aroma compounds \[7\]. These reactions interact with lipid oxidation products and Maillard reaction intermediates \[7\]. The Hodge scheme provides a framework for understanding the complex reaction sequences involved in Maillard reaction progression \[4\].

Plant-based meat alternatives accumulated both beneficial and harmful Maillard reaction products during processing. Total free heterocyclic amine levels increased from 4.74-6.63 ng/g in raw plant proteins to 5.81-20.23 ng/g in textured vegetable proteins after extrusion \[5\]. Notably, plant-based hamburger meats contained lower total heterocyclic amines (160.30 ng/g) compared to roast beef patties (381.30 ng/g) \[5\], attributed to better amino acid composition patterns in the meat products \[5\].

Beneficial products included melanoidins responsible for brown color formation and antimicrobial properties \[1, 4\], and pyrazines associated with desirable roasted aromas \[7\]. Myoglobin addition significantly increased aldehydes and decreased hydrocarbons \[7\], while grilling increased pyrazine content from 11.9 ± 3.0% to 16.1 ± 2.3% with 1.0% myoglobin supplementation \[7\]. Reductone intermediates, heterocyclic compounds, and melanoidins exhibited antioxidant and antibacterial activities \[3\].

Harmful products included heterocyclic amines, acrylamide, furans, and AGEs with known carcinogenic properties \[4\]. These compounds increase cytotoxicity and associate with chronic diseases \[3\]. The advanced stage of the Maillard reaction generated particularly problematic compounds including heterocyclic amines and acrylamide \[3\]. Furosine and pyridosine served as neutral marker compounds for assessing glycation quality \[1\].

## Processing Conditions and Their Influence

Processing conditions critically influenced Maillard reaction pathway progression and product formation in plant-based meat alternatives. Temperature emerged as the primary driver, with studies examining ranges from 40-80°C for controlled glycation \[1\] to 160-250°C for cooking applications \[5, 7\]. Higher temperatures and longer heating times enhanced reactivity \[1\], though excessive heating could lead to product degradation, as evidenced by decreased MeAαC content when cooking duration extended from 6 to 12 minutes at 160°C \[5\].

Extrusion processing, utilizing temperatures of 120-180°C for soy protein texturization \[7\], facilitated pyrazine formation via Maillard reactions \[7\]. Thermal extrusion cooking represented the primary structuring technique for transforming plant proteins into fibrous meat-like textures \[9\]. During extrusion, heterocyclic amine levels accumulated significantly \[5\].

pH conditions substantially affected reaction pathways, with slightly alkaline pH ranges favoring Maillard reactions \[1\]. Water activity levels between 0.5-0.8 represented optimal conditions for reaction progression \[1\], balancing molecular mobility with substrate concentration. Processing durations ranged from several hours to weeks depending on temperature \[1\], with shorter times at higher temperatures and longer periods required at lower temperatures.

Grilling at 250°C for 12 minutes transformed aroma precursors into volatile components and increased pyrazine content \[7\]. Myoglobin addition enhanced aroma precursor formation, with grilling subsequently transforming these precursors into volatiles \[7\]. The interaction between controlled temperature (below 60°C), humidity, water activity (0.5-0.7), and time (24-48 hours) determined optimal conditions for obtaining beneficial products while avoiding harmful compound formation \[4\].

Different substrate availability and pre-processing methods affected Maillard reaction product presence \[6\]. Novel processing approaches including ultrasonication and microwave-assisted Maillard conjugation demonstrated potential for enhancing functional properties \[4\].

Plant protein structure fundamentally influenced reaction pathways through several mechanisms. High molecular weight storage proteins with limited water solubility presented challenges \[2\], while amino acid composition determined reactivity patterns. Plant proteins exhibited richness in lysine, leucine, aspartic acid, and arginine but deficiencies in tryptophan and sulfur-containing methionine and cysteine \[1\]. This composition affected accessibility of reactive amino groups and stability of protein-carbohydrate complexes \[4\].

Pre-processing modifications of plant proteins altered Maillard reaction product formation \[6\]. Textured vegetable proteins created through extrusion showed different reactivity compared to raw plant proteins \[5\]. High-moisture and low-moisture protein extrudates with varying pea protein proportions demonstrated distinct Maillard reaction patterns \[6\].

Carbohydrate selection influenced reaction pathways and product profiles. Simple sugars like glucose initiated early-stage reactions \[1, 4\], while polysaccharides including dextran, maltodextrin, and specialty polysaccharides like lentinan and seaweed polysaccharides participated in protein glycation \[1\]. Polysaccharide-induced Maillard reactions modified protein structures and improved functional properties \[3\].

Myoglobin addition at 0.5-1.0% levels (mimicking endogenous myoglobin in meat) significantly affected volatile compound classes \[7\]. Myoglobin catalyzed lipid oxidation reactions that interacted with Maillard reaction pathways \[7\], notably increasing aldehydes and decreasing hydrocarbons while enhancing pyrazine formation \[7\].

## Functional Outcomes

Maillard reaction pathways produced diverse functional effects relevant to plant-based meat alternative performance and consumer acceptance. Protein functionality improvements included enhanced solubility, emulsification, and stability against environmental factors like pH shifts and salt addition \[1\]. Glycated plant proteins demonstrated improved thermal stability, pH sensitivity, emulsion stability, and surface hydrophobicity \[4\]. These modifications proved critical for achieving meat-like textures and processing stability \[4\].

Sensory impacts represented paramount functional outcomes. The Maillard reaction contributed to generating meat-like flavor compounds affecting taste and aroma properties \[8\]. Myoglobin addition enhanced the aromatic profile of plant-based meat alternatives by increasing aldehydes associated with meat-like aromas while decreasing hydrocarbons \[7\]. Grilling increased pyrazines contributing to roasted aromas \[7\]. Enhanced taste, aroma, and color in plant-based products improved consumer acceptance \[4\].

Nutritional and bioactivity changes included increased antioxidant activity through phenolic compound formation \[4\]. Melanoidins and certain neoglycoconjugates exhibited antimicrobial properties \[4\]. Reductone intermediates, heterocyclic compounds, and melanoidins demonstrated antioxidant and antibacterial activities \[3\]. However, harmful products including HMF, acrylamide, furans, and heterocyclic amines posed carcinogenic risks \[4\], increasing cytotoxicity and associating with chronic diseases \[3\].

Shelf-life effects manifested through better protection against physical and chemical degradation and reduced peptide degradation \[4\]. Maillard reaction products could enhance stability against thermal treatments and extreme pH conditions \[1\]. The balance between beneficial functional improvements and harmful product formation required careful control of reaction conditions and pathways.

## Control Strategies and Optimization

Multiple strategies emerged for controlling Maillard reaction pathways in plant-based meat alternatives. Temperature, time, pH, and water activity represented critical parameters for controlling yield and quality of glycated proteins \[1\]. Limiting reaction progression to early stages prevented unwanted advanced glycation end-products and melanoidins \[1\]. Optimal conditions included temperatures below 60°C and water activity between 0.5-0.7 \[4\], with reaction times of 24-48 hours \[4\].

Methods to enhance desirable pathways included myoglobin addition (0.5-1.0%) to catalyze lipid oxidation reactions and enhance Maillard browning \[7\]. Novel techniques like ultrasound treatment improved glycation efficiency \[1\]. Process modifications included grilling to transform precursors into volatile components \[7\], and controlled extrusion conditions to manage heterocyclic amine formation \[5\].

Approaches to minimize harmful product formation employed multiple mechanisms. Competing amino acids, bivalent cations, and enzymes controlled AGE formation \[4\]. Phytochemicals stopped initial progression toward Amadori rearrangement \[4\], mitigating oxidative reactions \[4\]. Avoiding acidic pH and minimizing thermal processing prevented unwanted pathway progression \[4\]. Addition of polyphenols, vitamins, and salts modified reactant behavior to reduce harmful products \[4\].

Ingredient modifications influenced reaction outcomes through reactant selection based on molecular weight to control reactivity \[1\] and targeting reactive sites through modification of reactants \[4\]. Interaction between proteins and polysaccharides improved functional properties and biological activity while requiring effective process control \[3\]. Pretreatment-assisted Maillard conjugation showed promise for optimization but required further research \[4\].

Monitoring strategies enabled pathway control. Furosine and pyridosine concentrations combined with browning index assessment measured reaction progress \[1\]. Advanced analytical techniques including UHPLC-MS/MS \[5\], headspace solid-phase microextraction followed by GC-MS \[7\], NMR-based metabolomics \[6\], and differential scanning calorimetry \[4\] quantified products and assessed structural changes. These methods identified critical processing stages requiring intervention for inhibiting Maillard reaction derivative harmful products \[5\].

## Synthesis

The literature revealed substantial heterogeneity in Maillard reaction pathways and outcomes across different plant-based meat alternative formulations and processing conditions, yet several patterns emerged explaining these variations. The apparent contradiction between studies showing beneficial functional improvements versus those emphasizing harmful product accumulation reflects fundamental differences in reaction stage control rather than conflicting evidence.

Studies finding beneficial outcomes typically limited reactions to early stages through controlled temperature (40-80°C) \[1, 4\], moderate water activity (0.5-0.7) \[1, 4\], and shorter processing times, producing functional glycoconjugates with improved solubility, emulsification, and antioxidant properties \[1, 4\]. Conversely, studies reporting harmful product formation examined higher-temperature cooking processes (160-250°C) \[5, 7\] necessary for consumer preparation, where advanced-stage reactions formed heterocyclic amines, acrylamide, and AGEs \[3–5\]. Both findings are valid within their respective processing margins: controlled glycation for ingredient preparation versus high-heat cooking for meal preparation.

The dose-response relationship between temperature and product formation exhibited non-linear characteristics. Studies demonstrated that extending cooking time from 6 to 12 minutes at 160°C initially increased MeAαC concentration from 29.23 to 59.44 ng/g \[5\], but further heating reduced levels to 42.26 ng/g \[5\], suggesting degradation pathways or volatilization at extended durations. Similarly, the effectiveness of myoglobin addition showed a dose-dependent enhancement, with pyrazine content increasing from 11.9% to 16.1% at 1.0% myoglobin supplementation \[7\], indicating optimization thresholds exist for pathway enhancement strategies.

Plant protein structure emerged as a critical confounder explaining variability across studies. Research using soy proteins \[1, 4, 7\] versus pea proteins \[1, 4, 6\] reported different Maillard reaction patterns attributable to distinct amino acid profiles. Plant proteins rich in lysine, leucine, aspartic acid, and arginine but low in sulfur-containing amino acids \[1\] exhibited different reactivity patterns than proteins with alternative compositions. Pre-processing methods including extrusion created textured vegetable proteins with altered reactive site accessibility \[5\], explaining variations in Maillard product formation even within the same protein source.

The interplay between substrate availability and processing history further reconciled apparent inconsistencies. Studies noting higher Maillard reaction marker levels in uncooked plant-based meat alternatives with lower formation during cooking \[6\] compared to conventional meat likely reflected pre-processing during ingredient manufacture (extrusion, texturization) that initiated early-stage reactions, leaving fewer reactive precursors available during final cooking. This mechanistic explanation predicts that extensively pre-processed plant proteins would show attenuated Maillard reaction progression during consumer cooking, whereas minimally processed formulations would exhibit more dramatic changes.

Quality hierarchy considerations suggest weighting primary experimental studies (Correia et al. 2024, Deng et al. 2022, Devaere et al. 2022\) more heavily than reviews when assessing specific processing conditions and quantitative outcomes, though reviews provide valuable synthesis of pathway mechanisms and control strategies. Studies with full-text access enabling detailed methodology assessment (Kutzli et al. 2020, Devaere et al. 2022, Naik et al. 2021\) offered more robust evidence for pathway mechanisms than abstract-only sources.

For plant-based meat alternative applications, the synthesis indicates that beneficial functional modifications can be achieved through controlled early-stage glycation at moderate temperatures (40-80°C), slightly alkaline pH, and optimal water activity (0.5-0.7) during ingredient preparation \[1, 4\]. However, subsequent consumer cooking at higher temperatures (160-250°C) inevitably progresses reactions into advanced stages forming heterocyclic amines and AGEs \[5, 7\]. Myoglobin addition at 0.5-1.0% levels offers a promising strategy to enhance desirable aromatic profiles during cooking \[7\], while ingredient selection (protein source, carbohydrate type) and pre-processing methods critically determine available pathways and product formation \[1, 6\]. Control strategies combining competing amino acids, bivalent cations, phytochemicals, and optimized pH prevent excessive harmful product accumulation while preserving functional benefits \[4\].
